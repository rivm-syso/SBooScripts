#### Size and Shape

The particle properties of the particle to be assessed can be defined by
either:

- `RadS` (radius of solid sphere) or

- `Shortest_side`, `Intermediate_side` or `Longest_side`.

These can be accompanied by the `Shape` for any particle that is not a
sphere and needs to be described by additional size characteristics: the
Intermediate and/or longest side.

Shapes implemented are:

- `Sphere` or `Default`

  - Defined by `RadS` (radius) or `Shortest_side` (diameter)

- `Ellipsoid`

  - Defined by `Shortest_side`, `Intermediate_side` and `Longest_side`

- `Cube` or `Box` or `Film`

  - Defined by `Shortest_side`, `Intermediate_side` and `Longest_side`

- `Cylindric - circular` or `Fiber`

  - Defined by `Shortest_side (diameter)` and `Longest_side`

- `Cylindric - elliptic`

  - Defined by `Shortest_side` (minor diameter), `Intermediate_side`
    (major diameter) and `Longest_side` (height).

If short, medium or longest side is not defined, it is defined as
RadS\*2 by default (as for a sphere) in ‘v_SettlingVelocity.R’.

These shapes and sizes are used for calculating:

- Volume (`fVol`)

- Surface Area (`f_SurfaceAreaParticle`)

- Perimeter (`f_PerimeterParticle`)

- Various shape factors for different drag methods (circularity,
  sphericity, elongation, etc.) in v_SettlingVelocity.R

#### Species size and density

Three different particle species exist: The nanoparticle or microplastic
particle (Soild/S), Aggregated to natural colloids (Small/A) and
Attached to coarse natural particulates (Large/P). The size and density
of these heteroagglomerates (Small/A or Large/P) is dependant on both
the size and density of the Solid/S species as well as those of the
other particle.

The density and radius of the nanoparticle/microplastic itself is given
by the substance properties RhoS and RadS/sides (see above). For the
aggregated and the properties of the “natural part” of the species;
*CoarseParticulate* for the Attached species and *NaturalColloid* for
the Aggregated species. Those properties are RhoCP and RhoCOL for
density and RadCP and RadCOL for their radius. An overview of excel
origins:

#### Settling velocity

An example of a process dependent on these characteristics is the
Settling Velocity. The Settling Velocity is calculated through the
choice of different drag methods (Dioguardi, Swamee, Stokes, Bagheri) of
which the original does not include drag.

Dittmar et al. (2024) (<https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.est.3c09602>)
reviewed multiple drag models (Waldschläger, Khatmullina, Dioguardi,
Bagheri, etc.). Bagheri & Bonadonna (2016)
(<https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0032591016303539>)
is generally recommended, as Coyle et al. (2023)
(<https://doi.org/10.1021/acsestwater.2c00466>) have shown that the
model was applicable to large microplastics, while Dittmar et al. (2024)
have shown its applicability to smaller MPs. Both articles show that the
model performs better than other available models for MPs, although the
model was developed for small particles in general, not microplastics.

The settling velocity is computed though the following functions:

- f_DragCoefficient: This function computes the drag coefficient (CD)
  according to the selected drag method (e.g.: DragMethod ==
  “Dioguardi”). The equations and inputs for CD depends on the drag
  method chosen. The inputs (Sphericity, etc.) are calculated in
  v_SettlingVelocity.R

- f_SetVelSolver: The settling velocity (vs) depends on the reynolds
  number (Re) and the drag coefficient. However, Re itself depends on
  the settling veloicity, therefore this solver function is needed to
  solve for the settling velocity, which solves for vs until it
  converges.

- v_SettlingVelocity: Computes the inputs needed (described above) to
  solve for the settling velocity in air and water.
